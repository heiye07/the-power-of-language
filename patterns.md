# Patterns

## Parallel Activation Analysis
**When to use**: Explaining multilingual attention, mistakes, creativity, or interference.
**How**: Identify the input cue, list same-language neighbors, cross-language neighbors, translations, and associated cultural concepts. Then ask which active path best explains the observed behavior.
**Trade-offs**: Captures richness but can over-explain if language proficiency, recency, and task context are ignored.

## Language-Dependent Recall
**When to use**: Interviews, therapy, legal testimony, education, or personal memory work involving multilingual people.
**How**: Ask what language was active during the original event. If recall is weak, try retrieval prompts in that language or in culturally linked settings.
**Trade-offs**: Useful cue, not a guarantee. Trauma, proficiency, and social safety can dominate.

## Foreign Language Distance
**When to use**: Moral, medical, financial, consumer, or risk decisions made in a non-native language.
**How**: Check whether the second language is reducing emotional salience, disgust, fear, ownership, or framing sensitivity. Re-run the decision in the native language if emotional stakes matter.
**Trade-offs**: Can reduce bias but may also blunt empathy or lived meaning.

## Label Audit
**When to use**: Policy, advertising, clinical communication, naming, UX copy, social categories, and research instruments.
**How**: Compare alternative labels and ask what each highlights, hides, moralizes, humanizes, or dehumanizes.
**Trade-offs**: Labels matter, but structural incentives and cultural context still matter.

## Difference-Not-Disorder Check
**When to use**: Assessing multilingual children, dialect speakers, immigrants, or patients in clinical/educational contexts.
**How**: Test in relevant languages, consult family/community norms, separate language difference from impairment, and avoid dominant-culture baselines as the only norm.
**Trade-offs**: Requires time and interpreters; shortcut diagnosis creates larger downstream harm.

## Translation Reconstruction
**When to use**: Translating humor, poetry, clinical information, political messaging, examples, or educational content.
**How**: Preserve function first: intended emotion, timing, cultural assumption, register, and decision effect. Then rebuild words and examples in the target language.
**Trade-offs**: Fidelity to function can require departure from literal wording.

## Multilingual Learning Loop
**When to use**: Designing language learning or symbolic learning pathways.
**How**: Reuse prior languages, scripts, sound patterns, grammar, memory techniques, and executive-control practice. Add consistent exposure and social use.
**Trade-offs**: Transfer helps most when learners notice the overlap and have enough input quality.

## Research Diversity Control
**When to use**: Designing psychological, educational, medical, or neuroscience studies.
**How**: Record language history, proficiency, age of acquisition, current use, dialect, literacy, and cultural context. Treat multilingualism as a variable, not noise.
**Trade-offs**: Adds complexity but improves validity and reproducibility.
