# Chapter 9: 翻译的发现

## Core Idea
Translation reveals that form and meaning are not fully arbitrary. Sounds, scripts, poetry, interpreting, and learning strategies show how language codes connect perception, culture, and cognition.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Sound-symbolism lens**
  - When to use: Naming, poetry, branding, language learning, and cognitive experiments.
  - How: Ask whether sound shape, mouth movement, rhythm, or visual form influences perceived meaning.
- **Translation as reconstruction**
  - When to use: Poetry, humor, legal/medical interpreting, cultural examples, or idioms.
  - How: Preserve function, emotion, register, and cultural fit rather than word-for-word mapping.
- **Language-learning overlap**
  - When to use: Teaching new words or designing learning paths.
  - How: Use known-language neighbors, frequency, concreteness, orthography, sound probability, and mnemonics.

## Key Concepts
- **Bouba-kiki effect**: People reliably map some sounds to visual shapes.
- **Onomatopoeia**: Words that imitate sounds, though they vary across languages.
- **Simultaneous interpreting**: Real-time listening, decoding, reformulating, and speaking in another language.

## Mental Models
- Good translation is closer to remaking an instrument than replacing strings one by one.
- Prior language knowledge reduces the amount of truly new information in the next language.

## Anti-patterns
- **Dictionary translation**: Assuming equivalent words reproduce equivalent meaning.
- **Humor literalism**: Translating joke content while losing timing and social context.
- **No-overlap teaching**: Ignoring the learner's existing linguistic resources.

## Worked Example
The bouba-kiki task shows that people often pair round sounds with round shapes and sharp sounds with jagged shapes. This suggests that some sound-meaning relationships are psychologically meaningful, even if most words are conventional.

## Key Takeaways
1. Translation is cognitively demanding because it must carry meaning, culture, timing, and emotion.
2. Interpreters develop specialized control networks and executive abilities.
3. Known languages make new languages easier to learn by overlap and control practice.
4. Poetry exposes the limits of literal translation most clearly.
