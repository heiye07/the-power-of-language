# Chapter 6: 不同的语言，不同的灵魂

## Core Idea
Different languages can activate different selves, emotions, memories, gestures, cultural frames, and moral choices.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Cultural frame switching**
  - When to use: Explaining cross-language personality, parenting, gesture, or consumer differences.
  - How: Ask which cultural norms and scripts the active language cues.
- **Emotion-language coupling**
  - When to use: Therapy, conflict, affection, trauma, or taboo language.
  - How: Compare native and second-language emotional intensity and bodily arousal.
- **Foreign language moral distance**
  - When to use: Moral dilemmas, medical risk, gambling, public health, or consumer judgment.
  - How: Check whether non-native language reduces affect and increases utilitarian or risk-tolerant choices.

## Key Concepts
- **OCEAN/CANOE traits**: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism.
- **Skin conductance response**: Physiological arousal measure used for emotional language effects.
- **Framing effect**: Decision shifts caused by how options are worded.

## Mental Models
- Treat language as a cue that brings one self to the foreground.
- For high-stakes decisions, rehearse the decision in both native and second languages.

## Anti-patterns
- **Single-personality scoring**: Assuming personality answers are language-invariant.
- **Literal bilingual consent**: Assuming comprehension means equal emotional resonance.

## Worked Example
In moral dilemmas, people using a foreign language are more likely to choose utilitarian options. The likely mechanism is reduced emotional immediacy, not superior morality.

## Key Takeaways
1. Language can change self-description, gesture, parenting behavior, and emotional arousal.
2. Native language often carries stronger affective charge.
3. Labels for emotion can regulate emotion.
4. Language choice matters in law, medicine, therapy, and intimate relationships.
