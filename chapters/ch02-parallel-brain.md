# Chapter 2: 大脑：并行处理的超级有机体

## Core Idea
The multilingual brain does not simply switch one language off and another on. Multiple languages remain active together, creating parallel activation and requiring powerful control systems.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Parallel activation**
  - When to use: Explaining cross-language interference, eye movements, accent effects, or multilingual attention.
  - How: List all languages that could be activated by sound, spelling, sign, object, or translation.
- **Ripple effect**
  - When to use: Explaining why a small word cue can trigger many related concepts.
  - How: Trace activation from word form to meanings, translations, neighbors, and associated concepts.
- **Dynamic activation threshold**
  - When to use: Explaining why some languages intrude more after recent use or when languages are similar.
  - How: Consider proficiency, recency, age of acquisition, similarity, and context.

## Key Concepts
- **Eye tracking**: Measuring gaze to infer unconscious activation.
- **Cross-language competition**: Activation of words or structures from a non-target language.
- **Domain-general transfer**: Language-control experience influencing non-language cognition.

## Mental Models
- Think of the multilingual brain as a live network, not a set of separate drawers.
- Treat interference as evidence of system design: the brain keeps options ready instead of waiting to identify language first.

## Anti-patterns
- **On/off bilingualism**: Assuming languages are fully disabled when not in use.
- **Noise framing**: Treating cross-language activation as error rather than information.

## Worked Example
When a Russian-English bilingual hears an English word such as `speaker`, gaze can drift toward an object whose Russian name overlaps in sound. The person is not choosing to translate; another language is active underneath the task.

## Key Takeaways
1. Parallel activation is efficient because it prepares the brain for unpredictable input.
2. Cross-language activation spans sounds, spellings, signs, syntax, meanings, and translations.
3. Managing competition trains attention and control.
4. Multilingual cognition is a changing state, not a fixed trait.
