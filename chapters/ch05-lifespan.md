# Chapter 5: 从童年到老年

## Core Idea
Multilingual experience affects the whole lifespan: it supports infant learning, childhood metalinguistic awareness, executive control, academic development, and cognitive reserve in aging.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Cognitive reserve**
  - When to use: Discussing dementia delay, aging, stress, or brain injury resilience.
  - How: Distinguish brain pathology from functional performance; multilingual networks can compensate.
- **Early metalinguistic advantage**
  - When to use: Evaluating bilingual children's development.
  - How: Look for awareness that objects and names are separable and that several labels can map to one concept.
- **Executive-control practice**
  - When to use: Explaining task switching, inhibition, attention, and bilingual education benefits.
  - How: Connect language selection and suppression to broader control mechanisms.

## Key Concepts
- **Cognitive reserve**: Functional resilience under decline.
- **Neural reserve**: Structural/functional reinforcement of brain networks.
- **Perceptual narrowing**: Infants lose sensitivity to unused language contrasts as native patterns strengthen.
- **Two-way immersion**: Schooling that teaches content through two languages to mixed-language groups.

## Mental Models
- Think of multilingualism as a daily cognitive workout that does not require separate gym time.
- Treat child bilingualism as enriched input, not confusion.

## Anti-patterns
- **Bilingual harm myth**: Claiming multiple languages cause language disorder or confusion.
- **One-language testing**: Underestimating bilingual children by counting only one language.
- **Late-start fatalism**: Assuming adults cannot benefit from language learning.

## Worked Example
In card-sorting and attention tasks, bilingual children often switch rules or ignore distractors more effectively because they practice selecting the relevant language while suppressing another.

## Key Takeaways
1. Multilingual experience can delay functional dementia symptoms by several years on average.
2. Bilingual children are not harmed by rich, high-quality input in more than one language.
3. Input quantity and quality matter more than enforcing monolingual purity.
4. Executive control benefits are real but context-sensitive, not magical.
