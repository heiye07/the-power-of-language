# Chapter 8: 文字之变

## Core Idea
Words, grammatical categories, names, accents, and language norms shape social perception. They can humanize, dehumanize, include, exclude, diagnose, or stigmatize.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Grammatical category effect**
  - When to use: Analyzing gendered nouns, animacy, pronouns, or nature language.
  - How: Ask how grammar classifies entities and what associations the classification triggers.
- **Name and accent bias audit**
  - When to use: Hiring, school admissions, clinical settings, media, and product design.
  - How: Check what identity assumptions are activated by names, accents, or dialect features.
- **Difference-not-disorder**
  - When to use: Speech-language assessment, multilingual education, and clinical intake.
  - How: Determine whether the pattern is expected in the person's language/dialect before pathologizing it.

## Key Concepts
- **Grammatical gender**: Noun classification that can alter description and memory.
- **Dialect difference**: A systematic language variety, not an inferior form.
- **Cultural competence**: Ability to work effectively with the patient's or learner's language and cultural norms.

## Mental Models
- Treat categories as behavioral nudges: even arbitrary grammar can recruit stereotypes.
- In assessment, first ask "different from whose norm?"

## Anti-patterns
- **Dominant-standard bias**: Treating mainstream speech as the only valid baseline.
- **Neutral-name fantasy**: Assuming names and pronouns do not affect evaluation.
- **Child interpreter dependence**: Using children as routine medical or legal interpreters.

## Worked Example
Speakers describe the same object differently when its grammatical gender differs across languages. A key can be described with more stereotypically masculine or feminine traits depending on the language category.

## Key Takeaways
1. Grammar can shape memory, description, and personification.
2. Names and accents activate race, gender, class, age, and competence assumptions.
3. Clinical and educational systems often misread multilingual difference as deficit.
4. Cultural and linguistic competence is ethical, practical, and economically important.
