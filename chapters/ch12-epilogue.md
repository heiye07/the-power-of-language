# Epilogue: 轻松愉快的尾声

## Core Idea
It is never too early or too late to learn a language. Earlier exposure helps, but adult learners can still benefit quickly; rich, consistent, meaningful input matters most.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Language attrition and savings**
  - When to use: Adoption, migration, heritage languages, and rediscovery of childhood language.
  - How: A lost language may leave traces that make relearning easier than first learning.
- **Adult language learning strategy**
  - When to use: Helping adults choose practical methods.
  - How: Combine courses, apps, travel, social connection, habit design, mnemonics, and personal fit.
- **Bilingual parenting input model**
  - When to use: Advising families raising bilingual children.
  - How: Maximize input quantity, input quality, multiple speakers, child interest, and sustainable family strategy.

## Key Concepts
- **Language attrition**: Reduced access to a language after disuse.
- **Critical period debate**: The contested claim that language learning sharply declines after a fixed age.
- **Input quality**: Rich, interactive, responsive language exposure.

## Mental Models
- Best language for a parent to use is the one that provides the richest interaction, not the one that sounds most prestigious.
- Language learning is habit plus social use, not just study.

## Anti-patterns
- **Perfect-fluency threshold**: Ignoring benefits unless the learner becomes fully fluent.
- **Screen-only child exposure**: Treating passive media as equivalent to human interaction.
- **Parent second-language pressure**: Asking parents to avoid their fluent mother tongue in favor of weaker input.

## Worked Example
For bilingual parenting, choose a family strategy such as one-parent-one-language, home-language/school-language, heritage classes, or community immersion. The right strategy is the one the family can sustain with warmth and rich interaction.

## Key Takeaways
1. Previously learned languages can leave recoverable traces.
2. Adults can learn languages and benefit from even short intensive exposure.
3. Children need abundant, high-quality, interactive input.
4. A bilingual child should first be a happy, supported child.
